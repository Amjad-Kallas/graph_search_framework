

The Six-Day War of June 1967 stands as a pivotal moment in modern Middle Eastern history, reshaping regional power dynamics and setting the stage for enduring conflicts. Its origins lie in a decade of escalating tensions following the 1948 Arab-Israeli War, which left Israel in control of territory it had been granted under the UN partition plan, while Egypt, Jordan, and Syria held the Gaza Strip, West Bank, and Golan Heights, respectively. By 1967, these borders remained unratified, and Arab states, led by Egypt’s Gamal Abdel Nasser, increasingly framed the conflict as a pan-Arab struggle against Israel’s existence. Nasser, seeking to bolster his image as a regional leader and counter domestic unrest, began mobilizing Egyptian forces along Israel’s southern border in May 1967, a move that alarmed Israeli leadership. Crucially, Egypt also demanded the withdrawal of United Nations Emergency Force (UNEF) peacekeepers from the Sinai Peninsula, a step that effectively removed a buffer zone between Egyptian and Israeli forces. More significantly, Egypt closed the Straits of Tiran to Israeli shipping, a blockade Israel viewed as an act of war under international law, as it cut off access to the vital port of Eilat. Israel’s government, under Prime Minister Levi Eshkol and Defense Minister Moshe Dayan, interpreted these actions as prelude to an imminent attack, fearing that Egypt, Jordan, and Syria—whose combined forces vastly outnumbered Israel’s—were preparing to overwhelm it. Intelligence reports suggested Arab armies were poised to strike, and Israel’s military doctrine emphasized preemptive action to survive existential threats. On June 5, 1967, Israel launched a surprise air assault, destroying most of Egypt’s air force on the ground within hours. This decisive opening allowed Israeli ground forces to rapidly advance into the Sinai Peninsula, defeating Egyptian troops in a series of swift battles. Meanwhile, Jordan, which had signed a defense pact with Egypt, entered the war after Israeli forces crossed into the West Bank. Israeli troops captured East Jerusalem and the West Bank, including the Old City, reunifying the city for the first time since 1948. In the north, Israel’s forces overran the Golan Heights from Syria after a fierce battle, securing a strategic vantage point overlooking northern Israel. The war concluded on June 10 with Israel in control of vast territories: the Sinai Peninsula, the West Bank, East Jerusalem, the Gaza Strip, and the Golan Heights. Arab casualties were heavy, with estimates of over 15,000 soldiers killed and thousands more captured, while Israel suffered around 800 fatalities. The swift victory, achieved through superior military planning and initiative, left Israel occupying territories that had been held by its neighbors since 1948, a reality that fundamentally altered the region’s geopolitical landscape. The war’s immediate aftermath saw the United Nations Security Council pass Resolution 242, calling for Israel’s withdrawal from occupied territories in exchange for peace treaties, though it avoided specifying exact borders. This ambiguity became a cornerstone of future negotiations. Crucially, the conflict also triggered the displacement of approximately 300,000 Palestinians from the West Bank and Gaza, many fleeing to Jordan, Lebanon, or Egypt, a demographic shift that intensified Palestinian national identity and fueled future resistance movements. Israel’s occupation of these territories, which it viewed as strategic security assets, created a long-term dilemma: how to govern populations under its control without granting them citizenship or political rights. The war’s legacy is thus deeply intertwined with the unresolved Israeli-Palestinian conflict, as the occupied territories became central to decades of negotiations, uprisings, and failed peace efforts. The Six-Day War also marked a turning point in Cold War dynamics, as the Soviet Union’s failed support for Arab states and Israel’s decisive victory bolstered Western influence in the region. While Israel achieved its immediate goal of securing its borders through military action, the territorial gains it inherited ultimately entrenched a conflict that continues to define Middle Eastern politics, demonstrating how a brief, decisive war could have consequences stretching far beyond its six-day duration. The war’s true significance lies not merely in its battlefield outcomes, but in how it established the framework for the region’s enduring struggles over land, identity, and sovereignty.

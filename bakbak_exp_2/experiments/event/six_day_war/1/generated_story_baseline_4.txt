

The Six-Day War of June 1967 emerged from decades of unresolved conflict in the Middle East, rooted in the 1948 Arab-Israeli War and the subsequent occupation of Palestinian territories by Jordan and Egypt. By 1967, tensions had reached a dangerous peak due to a combination of military posturing, political rhetoric, and a critical act of blockade. Egypt, under President Gamal Abdel Nasser, had been steadily mobilizing forces along Israel’s southern border in the Sinai Peninsula, a move widely interpreted as a threat. Crucially, Nasser also closed the Straits of Tiran to Israeli shipping, a decision that violated international law and effectively blockaded Israel’s only access to the Red Sea via the port of Eilat. This blockade, coupled with Egypt’s expulsion of UN peacekeeping forces from the Sinai and its military alliance with Jordan and Syria, created an atmosphere of existential crisis for Israel. Israeli leaders, believing a major Arab attack was imminent and that their survival depended on acting first, made the difficult decision to launch a preemptive strike.

On the morning of June 5, 1967, the Israeli Air Force executed a devastating surprise attack, destroying nearly the entire Egyptian air force on the ground within hours. This decisive action, known as Operation Focus, crippled Arab air defenses and secured Israeli air superiority for the entire conflict. With the skies cleared, Israeli ground forces advanced rapidly. In the Sinai Peninsula, Israeli troops overwhelmed Egyptian forces, capturing the entire region by June 8, including the vital port of Sharm. Simultaneously, Jordan, which had signed a defense pact with Egypt, launched artillery attacks on Israeli positions in Jerusalem and the West Bank. In response, Israeli forces crossed the Jordan River, capturing East Jerusalem—including the Old City and the Western Wall—and the entire West Bank territory by the end of the sixth day. Meanwhile, Syria, which had been shelling Israeli settlements from the Golan Heights, faced a swift Israeli counteroffensive. Israeli forces seized the strategically important Golan Heights, a mountainous plateau overlooking northern Israel, by June 10.

The war concluded with a ceasefire on June 10, 1967, after just six days of intense fighting. Israel’s victory was overwhelming and swift, achieved through superior military planning, intelligence, and the element of surprise. The territorial gains were immense: Israel now controlled the Sinai Peninsula, the Gaza Strip, the West Bank, East Jerusalem, and the Golan Heights. This expansion, while securing Israel’s immediate borders, also created a new and complex reality. Israel found itself administering large populations of Palestinians in the West Bank and Gaza, territories it had not previously held, and controlling the Golan Heights, which bordered Syria. The capture of East Jerusalem, home to significant Jewish, Muslim, and Christian holy sites, was particularly significant for Israel’s national identity.

The war’s immediate consequences reshaped the Middle East. The Arab states involved suffered a profound military and political humiliation, leading to internal unrest and a loss of credibility for Nasser’s pan-Arab leadership. For Israel, the victory was a strategic triumph but also a heavy burden, as it now faced the challenge of governing millions of Palestinians under military rule. The conflict also intensified the Palestinian national movement, as the occupation of the West Bank and Gaza gave rise to new political organizations seeking self-determination. Internationally, the war spurred diplomatic efforts, culminating in United Nations Security Council Resolution 242, which called for Israel’s withdrawal from territories occupied in the war in exchange for peace. However, the resolution’s vague language on borders and the unresolved status of the occupied territories became central points of contention for decades.

The Six-Day War remains pivotal because it transformed the regional balance of power and entrenched the territorial disputes that continue to define Middle Eastern politics. Israel’s victory secured its security in the short term but created the long-term dilemma of occupation, which has fueled ongoing conflict. The war also marked a turning point in Arab-Israeli relations, shifting the focus from direct military confrontation to a more complex struggle over territory, identity, and statehood, a legacy that continues to influence peace negotiations and regional stability to this day. Understanding the war requires recognizing that it was not merely a series of battles, but the culmination of years of failed diplomacy, strategic miscalculation, and the desperate pursuit of security by both sides.

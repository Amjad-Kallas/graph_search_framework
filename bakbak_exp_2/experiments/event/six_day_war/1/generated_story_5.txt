

By early June 1967, tensions between Israel and its Arab neighbors had reached a critical point. Egypt, under President Gamal Abdel Nasser, had expelled United Nations peacekeeping forces from the Sinai Peninsula and massed troops along Israel’s border, while also closing the Straits of Tiran to Israeli shipping—a move Israel viewed as an act of war. Jordan, Egypt’s ally, had signed a defense pact with Egypt, and Syria, meanwhile, had been shelling northern Israeli villages. Israel, fearing a coordinated Arab assault, chose preemptive military action. On June 5th, it launched a devastating air strike that crippled the Egyptian air force, setting the stage for a rapid ground offensive. The war would last only six days but would fundamentally reshape the Middle East.

On June 6th, the Israeli Defense Forces (IDF) launched their ground operations. In the Sinai Peninsula, the Battle of Abu Ageila became a pivotal early engagement. Abu Ageila, a fortified Egyptian position near the southern edge of the Sinai, was a critical defensive line guarding the route toward the Suez Canal. The Egyptians had constructed elaborate defenses, including sand dunes, minefields, and concrete bunkers, making it a formidable obstacle. Israeli forces, primarily the 7th Armored Brigade, attacked with a combination of tanks and infantry, exploiting gaps in the Egyptian lines and using flanking maneuvers to bypass the strongest points. The battle was intense, with heavy fighting through the night, but Israeli forces ultimately broke through by dawn. Capturing Abu Ageila was crucial because it opened the path for the IDF to advance deep into the Sinai, threatening Egypt’s main forces and accelerating the collapse of Egyptian resistance in the region. It demonstrated the IDF’s tactical flexibility and the vulnerability of the Egyptian defenses after the initial air campaign.

Meanwhile, on the western front, Israeli forces were advancing toward Jerusalem, a city divided since the 1948 Arab-Israeli War. East Jerusalem, including the Old City, had been under Jordanian control since that conflict. Jordan’s forces held a series of fortified positions along the city’s northern and western edges, with Ammunition Hill—a strategic, steeply sloping ridge on the western slope of Mount Scopus—being one of the strongest. The hill dominated the approach to Jerusalem and was heavily defended by Jordanian troops, with bunkers, trenches, and machine-gun nests. The battle for Ammunition Hill, fought on June 6th, was one of the most fiercely contested of the entire war. Israeli paratroopers, part of the 55th Paratroop Brigade, launched a determined assault, fighting hand-to-hand through the rocky terrain and crumbling walls of the hilltop position. The Jordanians, though outnumbered, put up a desperate resistance, making the capture of the hill extremely costly for the Israelis. After hours of brutal combat, the Israelis finally secured the position, opening the way for their forces to move into East Jerusalem. This victory was not only a military necessity but also deeply symbolic, as it allowed Israel to reunite Jerusalem under its control for the first time since 1948, a moment of profound significance for the Jewish state.

These two battles on June 6th exemplified the broader Israeli strategy: swift, decisive strikes against key enemy strongholds to shatter Arab lines and achieve rapid territorial gains. The capture of Abu Ageila enabled the IDF to push further into Egypt, while the fall of Ammunition Hill cleared the path to Jerusalem, a city of immense religious and political importance. The swift victories on the first day of the ground campaign set the tone for the war’s outcome. Within six days, Israel had defeated Egypt, Jordan, and Syria, capturing the Sinai Peninsula, the West Bank, East Jerusalem, and the Golan Heights. The Six-Day War ended not just with territorial expansion but with a profound shift in the regional balance of power, leaving the Arab states humiliated and setting the stage for decades of conflict and negotiation. The battles at Abu Ageila and Ammunition Hill were not merely tactical victories; they were the decisive hinges upon which the entire war turned, securing Israel’s survival and altering the map of the Middle East forever.

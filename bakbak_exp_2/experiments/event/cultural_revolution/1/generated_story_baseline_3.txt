

Following the devastating economic and social consequences of the Great Leap Forward, which caused widespread famine in the early 1960s, Mao Zedong grew increasingly concerned that the Chinese Communist Party was drifting toward revisionism and bureaucratic elitism, moving away from the revolutionary ideals of the founding era. Seeking to reassert his authority and purge perceived counter-revolutionary elements within the party and society, Mao launched the Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution in 1966. He framed it as a necessary struggle to prevent China from becoming a capitalist state like the Soviet Union, which he believed had abandoned true socialism under Khrushchev. Mao’s strategy relied on mobilizing the youth, particularly students, through the newly formed Red Guards, who were encouraged to challenge authority figures—teachers, officials, and intellectuals—whom they were told embodied "bourgeois" or "feudal" thinking. This approach aimed to bypass the party apparatus, which Mao distrusted, and reignite mass mobilization as a core revolutionary tactic.

The movement quickly escalated beyond Mao’s initial control. Red Guards targeted schools, universities, and cultural institutions, destroying artifacts, books, and religious sites deemed symbols of the old order. Intellectuals, artists, and party members with conservative leanings were publicly denounced, subjected to forced labor, or imprisoned, often based on accusations of "counter-revolutionary" behavior. The chaos deepened as rival factions of Red Guards emerged, leading to violent clashes across the country. By 1967, the situation had deteriorated so severely that Mao, fearing the movement was destabilizing the state, ordered the People’s Liberation Army to intervene and restore order. This military crackdown, though ending the most extreme phase of the chaos, did not halt the persecution; instead, it shifted the focus toward the Gang of Four, a radical faction led by Mao’s wife, Jiang Qing, who continued to promote ideological purity and suppress dissent until Mao’s death in 1976.

The Cultural Revolution’s human and cultural toll was immense. Millions suffered persecution, with estimates of deaths ranging from hundreds of thousands to millions, though exact figures remain contested due to the era’s secrecy. Traditional Chinese culture, including historical sites and artistic heritage, was severely damaged. The period also disrupted education and economic planning, leaving a generation of students without formal schooling and contributing to a prolonged economic slowdown. Crucially, the movement exposed deep fractures within the Communist Party itself, as different factions vied for power under the guise of ideological purity. After Mao’s death, his successors, notably Deng Xiaoping, distanced themselves from the Cultural Revolution’s excesses, acknowledging it as a period of grave error in the party’s history. The 1981 Resolution on Certain Questions in the History of Our Party, issued by the Communist Party, formally condemned the Cultural Revolution as a “severe setback” that caused “serious disasters and losses” to the country and the people, attributing its origins to Mao’s mistaken judgment and the misuse of power by radical elements.

The legacy of the Cultural Revolution profoundly shaped China’s subsequent trajectory. It reinforced the party’s later emphasis on stability, economic development, and pragmatic governance under Deng Xiaoping, who prioritized opening China to the global economy and rebuilding institutions weakened during the era. The trauma of the period also influenced a cultural shift toward valuing education and material progress over ideological fervor, though the official narrative remains cautious about detailed discussion. For the broader public, the Cultural Revolution serves as a pivotal reminder of the risks of unchecked political mobilization and the importance of institutional checks on power. Its lessons, though often unspoken in public discourse, continue to inform China’s approach to governance, emphasizing that revolutionary ideals must be balanced with practical state-building to avoid repeating the cycle of upheaval that marked those turbulent years. The movement’s end did not bring immediate healing but rather set the stage for a deliberate, long-term effort to rebuild national unity and focus on economic recovery.

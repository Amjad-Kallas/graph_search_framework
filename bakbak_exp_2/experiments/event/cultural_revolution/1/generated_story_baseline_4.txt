

The Cultural Revolution, launched by Chairman Mao Zedong in 1966, emerged from his deepening concern that China’s socialist revolution was being undermined by bureaucratic inertia and the rise of a new privileged class within the Communist Party. Following the failures of the Great Leap Forward, Mao believed that the Party elite had grown detached from the masses and was drifting toward revisionism, potentially betraying the revolution’s core ideals. To reignite revolutionary fervor and purge these perceived enemies, he mobilized youth through the Red Guards, student groups initially encouraged to challenge authority figures labeled as "bourgeois" or "revisionist." This movement quickly escalated beyond Mao’s original intentions, as the Red Guards targeted teachers, intellectuals, party officials, and anyone associated with pre-revolutionary culture or traditional practices, which Mao’s ideology deemed part of the "Four Olds"—old ideas, culture, customs, and habits. The campaign aimed to destroy the remnants of China’s past and reshape society through constant political struggle, but it unleashed widespread chaos as local power struggles and personal vendettas fueled violence and destruction across the country.

The early years, particularly 1966 to 1968, saw intense social upheaval. Schools and universities closed, cultural artifacts were destroyed, and public campaigns denounced individuals for their perceived ideological deviations. Many intellectuals and officials were subjected to public humiliation, forced labor, or imprisonment, while the economy suffered from disrupted production and transportation. The movement fractured the Party itself, as different factions vied for Mao’s favor, leading to violent clashes between rival Red Guard groups and later between the Red Guards and the military. By 1967, the chaos had become so severe that the military, under Lin Biao and later Zhou Enlai, intervened to restore order, declaring martial law in key cities like Shanghai and Beijing. This marked a shift from mass mobilization to a more controlled phase, where the army suppressed radical factions and began reasserting state authority. The army’s intervention was driven by the urgent need to prevent total societal collapse, not by alignment with Mao’s original goals, though it remained nominally under his leadership.

The movement gradually lost momentum after 1969, following Mao’s endorsement of a more moderate approach at the Ninth Party Congress. While the Red Guards were disbanded, the political purges continued at a lower intensity until Mao’s death in 1976. The Cultural Revolution left profound scars: millions suffered imprisonment, exile, or loss of livelihood, educational systems were devastated for over a decade, and social trust was deeply eroded. Crucially, the period exposed the limitations of Mao’s strategy to sustain revolution through mass mobilization, as it destabilized the very institutions meant to uphold socialism. The chaos also created a generation of young people—many educated in the 1960s—who later became key figures in China’s post-Mao reforms. After Mao’s death, his successors, notably Deng Xiaoping, explicitly rejected the Cultural Revolution as a period of "severe setbacks," acknowledging its role in causing immense suffering and economic stagnation. Deng’s subsequent reforms, beginning in 1978, focused on rebuilding the economy and re-establishing stability, effectively marking a decisive break from the revolutionary fervor of the Cultural Revolution era. The legacy of this period remains a critical reference point in China’s modern history, illustrating the dangers of conflating political ideology with social transformation and underscoring the importance of institutional stability in governance. It serves as a reminder that while revolutionary movements can reshape societies, their execution without clear safeguards risks causing irreversible harm to both people and the state.

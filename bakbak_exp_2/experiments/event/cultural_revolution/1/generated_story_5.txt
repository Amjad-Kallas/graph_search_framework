

The Cultural Revolution, a complex and deeply consequential period in modern Chinese history, unfolded between 1966 and 1976 under the leadership of Chairman Mao Zedong. Its origins lie in Mao’s profound anxiety about the direction of the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) and the nation he had helped build. After the failures of the Great Leap Forward (1958-1962), which caused a catastrophic famine, Mao perceived a growing trend of "revisionism" within the party—meaning he believed party officials were becoming too pragmatic, prioritizing economic development over revolutionary ideals and drifting toward capitalist influences. He feared the party was losing its revolutionary purity and that the socialist gains of the 1949 revolution were at risk. To reassert his authority and purge these perceived threats, Mao launched a sweeping social and political campaign designed to mobilize the masses, particularly the youth, against the party establishment.

The campaign began in 1966 with Mao’s "Big Character Poster," a public denunciation of party leaders he accused of deviating from true Marxist-Leninist principles. This ignited the formation of the Red Guards, student and youth militias inspired by Mao’s call to "bombard the headquarters" of the party’s leadership. These groups, initially backed by Mao, targeted party officials, intellectuals, teachers, and anyone deemed "bourgeois" or "counter-revolutionary." Schools and universities closed, and public humiliation, beatings, and even deaths became widespread as Red Guards attacked "the Four Olds"—old ideas, culture, customs, and habits. The chaos escalated rapidly, as the military was initially hesitant to intervene, allowing the movement to spiral beyond Mao’s original control. The party’s central leadership fractured, with figures like Liu Shaoqi and Deng Xiaoping, who had been instrumental in post-revolution governance, purged and imprisoned.

By 1967, the violence intensified as rival Red Guard factions clashed, and the military, under Lin Biao, was eventually called in to restore order. This marked a shift: the campaign moved from chaotic popular rebellion to a more controlled, state-directed purge. The military’s intervention, however, did not end the turmoil; it merely redirected it. The Cultural Revolution became a tool for factional struggles within the party itself, with competing groups vying for Mao’s favor. Intellectuals, artists, and professionals faced severe persecution, while rural communities were swept into mass mobilization campaigns. The economic consequences were severe, with agricultural and industrial output plummeting as factories shut down and skilled workers were sent to the countryside for "re-education." Millions endured forced labor, public criticism sessions, and the destruction of cultural heritage, including ancient temples, artworks, and historical texts.

The movement’s intensity began to wane after Mao’s health declined in the early 1970s, and his death in 1976 triggered a swift political reversal. The Gang of Four, a radical faction Mao had empowered but who had become increasingly extreme, was arrested shortly after his passing. The new leadership, under Deng Xiaoping, who had been purged during the Cultural Revolution, initiated a period of reform and economic opening, explicitly rejecting the movement’s excesses. The Cultural Revolution left a profound legacy: it shattered social trust, caused immense human suffering, and delayed China’s modernization for a decade. Its history remains a subject of careful reflection in China, where it is now officially characterized as a period of "severe setbacks" and "great calamity" that the nation has worked to overcome. The events of this era underscore the dangers of ideological extremism and the critical importance of institutional stability in governance, lessons that continue to resonate in China’s ongoing development.

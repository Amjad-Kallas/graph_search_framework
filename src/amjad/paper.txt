In the late 18th century, France was a powder keg of staggering inequality. The nation was bankrupt from funding foreign wars, yet King Louis XVI and the nobility lived in opulence while the common people faced famine and crushing taxes. Driven by the Enlightenment ideals of liberty and equality, the desperate populace reached their breaking point.

The revolution ignited on July 14, 1789, when an enraged Parisian mob stormed the Bastille, a medieval fortress and symbol of royal tyranny. This decisive act sparked a nationwide uprising. The revolutionaries soon drafted the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen, demanding universal rights and stripping the clergy and nobility of their feudal privileges. The primary purpose was clear: to dismantle absolute monarchy and replace it with a representative government built on the principles of "Liberty, Equality, Fraternity."

However, the quest for a republic quickly plunged into chaos. Facing internal divisions and external threats from neighboring monarchies, the revolution radicalized. King Louis XVI and Queen Marie Antoinette were convicted of treason and sent to the guillotine in 1793. This ushered in a bloody, paranoid period known as the Reign of Terror, orchestrated largely by Maximilien Robespierre. Thousands of perceived enemies of the revolution were executed in the name of protecting the new republic.

The Terror ended with Robespierre’s own execution in 1794, leading to a more moderate but highly unstable government called the Directory. After a decade of political turmoil and economic instability, the French people yearned for order. The revolution ultimately concluded in 1799 when a popular and ambitious military general, Napoleon Bonaparte, staged a coup d'état. While Napoleon soon established a new authoritarian regime, the French Revolution successfully shattered the foundations of the old feudal order, permanently proving that the absolute power of kings could be broken.

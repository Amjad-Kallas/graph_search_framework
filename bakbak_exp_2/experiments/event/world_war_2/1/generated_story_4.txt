

World War II began not with a single battle but a grim sequence that unfolded across continents, its origins rooted in the aggressive expansionism of Nazi Germany and Imperial Japan. On September 3, 1939, the Battle of the Atlantic commenced, marking the longest continuous military campaign of the war. German U-boats targeted Allied shipping across the Atlantic, Arctic, and Caribbean, aiming to sever Britain’s lifelines and starve it into submission. This relentless pressure, combined with the swift German invasion of Poland on September 1, set the stage for a global conflict. The Soviet Union, bound by the secret Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact, invaded Poland from the east on September 17, sealing the country’s fate by October 6. This joint German-Soviet attack, known as the invasion of Poland, is widely recognized as the war’s true starting point, triggering Britain and France’s declarations of war.

The early years saw the Western Allies reeling. After the rapid fall of France in 1940, British and Allied forces were evacuated from Dunkirk in May–June, a desperate operation that saved over 300,000 troops but left Britain vulnerable. Nazi Germany then launched the Battle of Britain in July, seeking air superiority to enable a cross-Channel invasion. The Royal Air Force’s defense, bolstered by radar and skilled pilots, thwarted the Luftwaffe’s assaults. This victory, followed by the Blitz—eight months of sustained bombing of British cities—forced Germany to abandon plans for an invasion. Meanwhile, the Eastern Front became a crucible of brutal warfare. Germany’s invasion of the Soviet Union in June 1941 led to the siege of Leningrad, where civilians endured starvation and bombardment for 872 days, and the pivotal Battle of Stalingrad, which began in August 1942. There, Soviet forces encircled and destroyed the German Sixth Army, marking the war’s turning point in Europe.

In the Pacific, Japan’s surprise attack on Pearl Harbor in December 1941 thrust the United States into the conflict. Early Japanese advances, including the capture of Singapore in February 1942, seemed unstoppable until the Battle of Midway in June 1942. American codebreakers anticipated the Japanese fleet’s move, leading to the destruction of four Japanese aircraft carriers—a blow from which Japan never recovered. This victory shifted naval dominance to the Allies. On the Eastern Front, the Soviet Union’s counteroffensive at Prokhorovka in July 1943, part of the Battle of Kursk, saw the largest tank battle in history and shattered Germany’s last major offensive capability. The Allies also turned the tide in North Africa with Operation Torch in November 1942, landing in French Morocco and Algeria to push Axis forces toward Tunisia.

1944 brought decisive Allied offensives. The D-Day landings in Normandy on June 6 opened a second front in Western Europe, followed by Operation Cobra, which broke the German lines and led to the liberation of Paris. Simultaneously, the Soviets launched a massive push into Romania and Hungary, forcing Axis allies to switch sides. In the Pacific, the Battle of Leyte Gulf in October 1944 destroyed Japan’s remaining carrier strength, while the brutal battles of Imphal and Kohima in India repelled Japanese attempts to invade the subcontinent. The Battle of the Bulge, Germany’s final major offensive in December 1944, failed to split Allied forces, hastening the collapse of the Third Reich. By early 1945, Soviet troops encircled Budapest, and the Allies cleared the Scheldt estuary to open the vital port of Antwerp.

The war’s end approached rapidly. In May 1945, Germany surrendered unconditionally after Soviet forces captured Berlin. In the Pacific, the U.S. recaptured Guam and engaged in the bloody Battle of Manila in early 1945. The Soviet Union’s lightning invasion of Manchuria on August 9, 1945, overwhelmed Japanese forces there, while atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki forced Japan’s surrender on August 15. The global conflict, which began with the invasion of Poland and the Battle of the Atlantic, concluded with the Allied victory reshaping the world order. The war’s scale, from the frozen streets of Leningrad to the Pacific islands, underscored the devastating cost of ideological extremism and the resilience of those who resisted it.

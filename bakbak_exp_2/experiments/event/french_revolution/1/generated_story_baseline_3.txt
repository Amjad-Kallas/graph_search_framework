

The French Revolution, a pivotal event in world history, did not erupt suddenly but emerged from deep-seated tensions that had built for decades. By the late 1780s, France faced a severe financial crisis, driven by costly wars including support for the American Revolution, an inefficient and regressive tax system that placed the heaviest burden on the common people while exempting the nobility and clergy, and a growing population straining resources. The monarchy, under King Louis XVI, struggled to reform the system without alienating the privileged classes who controlled the Estates-General, the national assembly representing the three estates of society. When the king finally summoned this assembly in 1789 to address the crisis, the Third Estate—the commoners, comprising 98% of the population—demanded voting by head, not by estate, to reflect their numerical majority. The nobility and clergy resisted, leading the Third Estate to declare itself the National Assembly on June 17, 1789, asserting it alone could represent the nation. This act, reinforced by the famous Tennis Court Oath a week later where members vowed not to disband until France had a constitution, marked the collapse of the old order and the birth of popular sovereignty.

The revolution gained unstoppable momentum as widespread unrest spread. Food shortages and rising bread prices fueled anger among urban workers and peasants, while the monarchy’s hesitation to support reform and attempts to deploy troops near Paris were seen as threats to the Assembly. On July 14, 1789, a crowd stormed the Bastille, a royal fortress in Paris, seeking weapons and ammunition but also symbolizing the tyrannical power of the monarchy. This event, though militarily insignificant, became a powerful emblem of the people’s victory over oppression. The National Assembly, now backed by popular pressure, moved swiftly to dismantle the feudal system. In August 1789, it abolished feudal privileges, ending the nobility’s tax exemptions and manorial rights, and issued the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen, enshrining principles of liberty, equality, and popular sovereignty as the foundation of governance. These reforms aimed to address the core grievances of the common people: economic injustice and political exclusion.

However, the revolution soon faced internal divisions and external threats. The monarchy’s attempted flight to Austria in June 1791, seeking foreign intervention, shattered trust and radicalized the movement. France declared war on Austria and Prussia in 1792, fearing counter-revolution, which in turn intensified fears within the country and led to the overthrow of the monarchy. The monarchy was abolished, and France became a republic in September 1792. The radical Jacobin faction, led by Maximilien Robespierre, seized power in 1793, establishing the Committee of Public Safety to defend the revolution against internal dissent and foreign invasion. This period, known as the Reign of Terror, saw mass executions of perceived enemies, including the king and queen, as the government prioritized survival over stability. The Terror ended with Robespierre’s fall in July 1794, leaving a legacy of trauma but also of profound political change.

The revolution’s core achievements endured despite its turmoil. It dismantled the feudal order, established legal equality, and spread Enlightenment ideals of citizenship and rights across Europe. While the Directory, the government that followed the Terror, proved unstable, the revolution’s principles had fundamentally altered the political landscape. It demonstrated that popular sovereignty could replace divine-right monarchy and inspired movements for liberty worldwide. The revolution’s complexity—its blend of idealism, violence, and pragmatic state-building—showed that transforming a society required navigating deep social divisions and external pressures. Its most lasting impact was not the specific institutions it created, but the irrevocable shift it represented: the idea that governments derive power from the people, not from tradition or divine mandate, and that citizens could actively shape their own political destiny. This foundational shift in the relationship between state and society remains one of the revolution’s most significant legacies.

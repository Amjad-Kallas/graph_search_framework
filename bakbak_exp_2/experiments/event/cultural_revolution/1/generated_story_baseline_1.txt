

The Cultural Revolution, initiated by Mao Zedong in 1966 and lasting until his death in 1976, was a complex political movement driven by Mao’s deepening anxieties about the direction of the Chinese Communist Party and the nation. Following the failures of the Great Leap Forward, which caused a devastating famine, Mao believed that the Party had become increasingly bureaucratic, detached from the masses, and susceptible to "bourgeois" or revisionist influences that threatened socialist ideals. He feared that the Party elite, including figures like Liu Shaoqi and Deng Xiaoping, were promoting policies that prioritized economic development over revolutionary purity, potentially leading China down a capitalist path. To reassert his authority and purge these perceived threats, Mao mobilized the youth, particularly students, through the formation of the Red Guards. These groups were encouraged to attack the "Four Olds"—old ideas, culture, customs, and habits—as symbols of the old order, aiming to dismantle traditional values and institutional structures that he believed hindered revolutionary fervor.

The movement began with intense student activism, as Red Guards targeted teachers, intellectuals, and party officials labeled as "class enemies" or "capitalist roaders." Schools and universities shut down, and public campaigns denounced individuals for their backgrounds, writings, or perceived ideological deviations. This phase quickly escalated into widespread social chaos, as different factions within the Red Guards and later workers’ militias clashed violently. The destruction of cultural heritage, including ancient temples, books, and artworks, was extensive, driven by the belief that such elements represented oppressive traditions. The government’s authority fragmented as local power structures collapsed, and violence became pervasive, with many people subjected to public humiliation, forced labor, or imprisonment. Mao’s wife, Jiang Qing, and her allies, later known as the Gang of Four, gained significant influence during this period, promoting radical policies that intensified the turmoil.

By 1967, the chaos had spiraled beyond Mao’s initial control, threatening national stability and economic collapse. The military, under Lin Biao and later Zhou Enlai, intervened to restore order, declaring martial law in many cities and suppressing the most extreme Red Guard factions. This marked a shift from mass mobilization to a more controlled, albeit still repressive, phase. Zhou Enlai, while navigating the political minefield, managed to mitigate some of the worst excesses by protecting key officials and easing the impact on the economy. The movement also saw internal Party struggles, most notably the failed coup attempt by Lin Biao in 1971, which further destabilized the leadership. Despite Mao’s continued endorsement, the Cultural Revolution’s radical phase gradually waned as the Party leadership, including Deng Xiaoping who was rehabilitated in the mid-1970s, began to recognize its destructive consequences.

After Mao’s death in 1976, the Gang of Four were swiftly arrested, signaling the official end of the Cultural Revolution. The Chinese Communist Party formally condemned the period in 1981, acknowledging it as a "severe setback" that caused immense suffering, including the persecution of tens of millions of people and the devastation of cultural and educational institutions. The movement’s legacy was pivotal in shaping China’s post-Mao trajectory. It exposed the dangers of personality cults and ideological extremism, leading to the pragmatic economic reforms spearheaded by Deng Xiaoping in the late 1970s. These reforms deliberately distanced China from the Cultural Revolution’s radicalism, prioritizing development and stability over ideological purity. Understanding the Cultural Revolution thus requires recognizing it not merely as a period of chaos, but as a critical juncture where Mao’s fears of ideological deviation led to a protracted crisis that ultimately forced the Party to reassess its approach to governance and modernization, setting the stage for China’s transformation into a global economic power.

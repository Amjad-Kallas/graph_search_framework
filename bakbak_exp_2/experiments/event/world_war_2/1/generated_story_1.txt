

The war began not with a single battle but with a relentless campaign, the Battle of the Atlantic, which stretched from 1939 to 1945 and became the longest continuous military operation of the conflict. German U-boats targeted Allied supply convoys across the Atlantic, Arctic, and Caribbean, aiming to strangle Britain’s lifelines. This naval struggle set the stage for a global war that would soon engulf Europe, Asia, and the Pacific. The invasion of Poland on September 1, 1939, by Nazi Germany marked the official start, followed swiftly by the Soviet Union’s entry from the east on September 17, as agreed under the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact. This joint invasion crushed Poland by October 6, demonstrating the devastating efficiency of blitzkrieg tactics and signaling the collapse of the European order. As Germany turned westward, the Battle of France in 1940 saw Allied forces pushed to the coast at Dunkirk. The miraculous evacuation of over 338,000 British and French troops between May and June 1940, though a military retreat, preserved the core of the Allied fighting force and became a symbol of resilience. Germany then shifted focus to the skies with the Battle of Britain, a brutal aerial campaign from July to October 1940. The Royal Air Force’s defense of British airspace, aided by radar and superior fighter planes, thwarted Hitler’s planned invasion, forcing Germany to abandon Operation Sea Lion and shifting the war’s momentum. The Blitz, the sustained bombing of British cities from September 1940 to May 1941, aimed to break civilian morale but instead hardened national resolve, proving the limits of air power alone in achieving strategic victory.

Meanwhile, the Eastern Front became a brutal crucible. After Germany invaded the Soviet Union in June 1941, the siege of Leningrad began, trapping the city for 872 days and causing over a million civilian deaths from starvation and bombardment. The tide turned decisively at Stalingrad in late 1942, where Soviet forces encircled and destroyed the German Sixth Army, marking the war’s pivotal moment. The following summer, the Battle of Prokhorovka saw the largest tank clash in history, halting Germany’s last major offensive at Kursk and cementing Soviet dominance on the Eastern Front. In the Pacific, the U.S. Navy delivered a stunning blow at Midway in June 1942, sinking four Japanese aircraft carriers and ending Japan’s naval supremacy. This victory enabled the Allies to launch island-hopping campaigns, culminating in the fierce battles of Imphal and Kohima in 1944, which repelled Japan’s invasion of India and shattered its offensive capability. The Allies also turned the tide in Europe with Operation Torch, the November 1942 invasion of French North Africa, which opened a second front against Axis forces in the Mediterranean. By 1943, the Allies invaded Sicily and Italy, forcing Mussolini’s fall and drawing Germany deeper into a two-front war. The Battle of the Atlantic reached a climax in March 1943, when Allied convoy tactics and technology finally overwhelmed U-boats, securing vital supply lines for the coming invasion of Europe.

The Normandy landings on June 6, 1944, known as D-Day, launched the long-awaited Allied assault on Nazi-occupied Western Europe. After fierce fighting to secure a foothold, Operation Cobra in July broke the German defenses in Normandy, leading to the liberation of Paris. Meanwhile, in the Pacific, the Battle of Leyte Gulf in October 1944 destroyed Japan’s remaining naval strength, paving the way for the invasion of the Philippines. The war’s final year saw desperate German counterattacks like the Battle of the Bulge in December 1944, which failed to reverse Allied advances, and the Soviet capture of Budapest in February 1945. As the Allies closed in from both east and west, the Soviet Union launched a lightning invasion of Japanese-held Manchuria on August 9, 1945, shattering Japan’s last major defensive line and hastening its surrender. The Battle of Manila, the liberation of the Philippines, and the Soviet entry into the Pacific theater collectively sealed the Axis’ fate, bringing an end to the deadliest conflict in human history. By August 1945, the world faced a new reality, shaped by the immense sacrifices and the dawn of a divided Cold War era.

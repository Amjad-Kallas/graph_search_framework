

The Six-Day War of June 1967 emerged from decades of unresolved conflict between Israel and its Arab neighbors, rooted in competing nationalisms, territorial disputes, and deep-seated hostility following Israel's 1948 founding. By 1967, tensions had reached a critical point. Egypt, under President Gamal Abdel Nasser, had expelled UN peacekeeping forces from the Sinai Peninsula bordering Israel and massed troops there, while closing the Straits of Tiran to Israeli shipping – a move Israel viewed as an act of war, as it cut off access to the Red Sea and vital trade routes. Simultaneously, Nasser signed a defense pact with Jordan, which had controlled East Jerusalem and the West Bank since 1948, and Syria, which held the Golan Heights overlooking northern Israel. Arab leaders publicly declared their intent to destroy Israel, and military forces gathered along Israel's borders, creating a palpable sense of imminent attack. Israel's government, led by Prime Minister Levi Eshkol, faced intense pressure to act, fearing a coordinated assault that could overwhelm its smaller military. Intelligence suggested Egypt was preparing to strike, and Israel's leadership concluded that a preemptive strike was the only viable option to survive.

On the morning of June 5, 1967, Israel launched a massive surprise air assault against Egyptian airfields, destroying the bulk of Egypt's air force on the ground within hours. This decisive action, known as Operation Focus, crippled Arab air power and secured Israeli air superiority for the entire conflict. With the skies cleared, Israeli ground forces rapidly advanced. In the Sinai Peninsula, Israeli troops, employing mobile tactics, pushed deep into Egyptian territory, encircling and capturing the Egyptian Third Army near the Suez Canal by June 8. This swift victory secured the entire Sinai and gave Israel control of the Sinai Peninsula, the Gaza Strip, and access to the Red Sea. Meanwhile, Jordan, which had joined the war in response to Egyptian appeals and to reclaim East Jerusalem, fired artillery into Israeli Jerusalem. Israel responded by capturing East Jerusalem, including the Old City and its holy sites, on June 7, effectively reunifying the city for the first time since 1948 and bringing the West Bank under Israeli control. Syria, attempting to support Jordan from the Golan Heights, faced Israeli forces that swiftly overran the strategic high ground, capturing the Golan Heights by June 10. The war concluded with a ceasefire on June 10, leaving Israel in control of vast territories: the Sinai Peninsula, the West Bank (including East Jerusalem), the Gaza Strip, and the Golan Heights.

The immediate consequences were profound. Israel had achieved a stunning military victory, but it also found itself occupying millions of Palestinians in the West Bank and Gaza, and Syrians in the Golan Heights, territories it had not previously controlled. This occupation created a complex political and humanitarian reality, with the Palestinian population under Israeli military rule and no clear path to statehood. The war also triggered a massive refugee crisis, as hundreds of thousands of Palestinians fled the newly occupied territories. Regionally, the conflict shattered the Arab world's sense of collective strength, discrediting Nasser's leadership and fueling internal political shifts. Internationally, the war intensified the Cold War dynamic in the Middle East, with the Soviet Union backing the Arab states and the United States increasingly aligning with Israel. The United Nations passed Resolution 242, calling for Israel's withdrawal from territories it had captured and the recognition of Arab states' sovereignty, but the resolution's ambiguity on the extent of withdrawal and the lack of immediate implementation left the core issues unresolved. The war fundamentally altered the regional map and set the stage for decades of subsequent conflict, peace efforts, and diplomatic challenges, as the occupied territories became the central issue in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict and Arab-Israeli relations. The Six-Day War demonstrated the vulnerability of Arab military coalitions and the strategic advantage of Israeli military preparedness, but it also planted the seeds for enduring geopolitical tensions that continue to shape the Middle East today.

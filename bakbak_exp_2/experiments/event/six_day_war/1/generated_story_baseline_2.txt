

The Six-Day War of June 1967 marked a dramatic and decisive turning point in the modern Middle East, reshaping regional power dynamics and setting the stage for conflicts that continue to this day. Its origins lay in a deepening crisis of mutual distrust and escalating rhetoric between Israel and its Arab neighbors, primarily Egypt (then the United Arab Republic), Jordan, and Syria. For years, Israel had faced periodic border clashes and threats from the surrounding states, but tensions reached a critical peak in the spring of 1967. Egyptian President Gamal Abdel Nasser, seeking to bolster his standing in the Arab world and counter perceived Israeli aggression, took several provocative steps. He expelled the United Nations Emergency Force (UNEF) peacekeeping troops from the Sinai Peninsula, which had been stationed there since the 1956 Suez Crisis, and began amassing Egyptian forces along Israel’s border. Crucially, he also closed the Straits of Tiran to Israeli shipping, a move Israel viewed as an act of war that threatened its vital access to the Red Sea and its economic lifeline through the port of Eilat. Simultaneously, Nasser signed a mutual defense pact with Jordan, and Syrian forces began shelling Israeli settlements from the Golan Heights. Arab leaders publicly declared their intention to destroy Israel, and Nasser’s rhetoric, coupled with the concentration of Egyptian troops near the Israeli border, convinced Israeli leaders that a full-scale invasion was imminent.

Faced with what they perceived as an existential threat, the Israeli government, under Prime Minister Levi Eshkol and Defense Minister Moshe Dayan, made the fateful decision to launch a preemptive strike. On the morning of June 5, 1967, the Israeli Air Force executed Operation Focus, a surprise attack that destroyed nearly the entire Egyptian air force on the ground within hours. This stunning aerial victory crippled Egypt’s military capability and denied the Arab coalition air superiority, a critical factor in the rapid Israeli advances that followed. Within a day, Israeli ground forces crossed into the Sinai Peninsula, overwhelming Egyptian defenses through a combination of speed, tactical brilliance, and the shock of the initial air assault. By June 7, Israeli troops had captured the entire Sinai Peninsula and the strategically vital city of El Arish. Meanwhile, Jordan, which had joined the war after being pressured by Nasser, launched attacks against Israeli positions in Jerusalem and the West Bank. Israeli forces swiftly counterattacked, capturing East Jerusalem, including the Old City and the Western Wall, and the entire West Bank from Jordanian control by June 7. On the northern front, Israeli forces advanced into the Golan Heights, a strategically elevated area overlooking northern Israel that had been under Syrian control since 1948, defeating Syrian forces and securing the region by June 10.

The war concluded with a ceasefire on June 10, 1967, after just six days of intense fighting. Israel achieved a stunning military victory, capturing vast territories: the Sinai Peninsula from Egypt, the West Bank and East Jerusalem from Jordan, and the Golan Heights from Syria. This expansion more than tripled Israel’s size, bringing its population under its control to over a million Palestinians, including those in the West Bank and Gaza Strip. The swift and decisive nature of the Israeli victory, achieved with relatively low casualties compared to the Arab forces, was a profound shock to the region and the world. For Israel, the war was a moment of immense relief and strategic security, eliminating the immediate threat of annihilation. However, the occupation of these territories, particularly the West Bank and Gaza, created a new and enduring challenge: the governance of a large Palestinian population under Israeli military rule. This situation, combined with the unresolved political status of the captured lands, became the central issue in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict for decades to come. The war also cemented Israel’s military reputation and significantly altered the balance of power in the Middle East, leaving Egypt, Jordan, and Syria humiliated and setting the stage for future diplomatic efforts, including the Camp David Accords and the Oslo Peace Process, which grappled with the consequences of Israel’s territorial gains. The Six-Day War thus remains a pivotal event, not merely for the brief conflict itself, but for the complex political landscape it forged, where the legacy of those six days continues to influence regional stability and international diplomacy.

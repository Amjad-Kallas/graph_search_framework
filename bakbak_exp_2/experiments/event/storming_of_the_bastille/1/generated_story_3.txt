

In the spring of 1789, France stood at a precipice. Decades of financial crisis, exacerbated by costly wars and an inequitable tax system that burdened the common people while exempting the nobility and clergy, had created a deep well of public anger. King Louis XVI, facing bankruptcy, reluctantly called the Estates-General—a gathering of the three estates of French society—to Versailles in May 1789, hoping to find a solution. The First Estate (clergy) and Second Estate (nobility) met separately, often blocking reforms favored by the Third Estate, which represented the vast majority of the population—commoners, merchants, and peasants. Frustrated by the king’s refusal to grant them voting power proportional to their numbers, the Third Estate members declared themselves the National Assembly on June 17th, vowing to draft a constitution for France. When the king locked them out of their usual meeting hall, they famously gathered in a nearby indoor tennis court at Versailles on June 20th. There, under the watchful eyes of the king’s guards, they took the Tennis Court Oath, pledging not to disband until France had a written constitution. This act of defiance, witnessed by a small crowd outside the hall, was a seismic moment: it declared the people, not the monarch, held sovereignty.

The oath was a direct challenge to the absolute power of the monarchy, but it also created immediate tension. The king, still resistant, ordered troops to gather near Paris, fueling widespread panic among the populace. Rumors spread like wildfire that the king planned to crush the National Assembly and arrest its leaders. On July 11th, he dismissed his popular finance minister, Jacques Necker, who had been seen as sympathetic to the Third Estate’s demands. This move was interpreted as a clear signal of royal hostility. Paris, already simmering with anxiety over food shortages and unemployment, erupted into chaos. The city’s citizens, many of whom were armed with hunting rifles and pikes, began to organize. They saw the Bastille, a massive fortress prison in the heart of Paris, not primarily as a place of detention but as a symbol of royal tyranny and a storehouse of weapons and gunpowder. The Bastille had been used for decades to imprison political opponents without trial, making it a potent emblem of the old regime’s arbitrary power.

By July 14th, the crowd’s focus turned to the Bastille. A group of about 800 Parisians, led by the radical leader the Marquis de Launay, the fortress’s governor, marched toward the fortress. The governor, misunderstanding the crowd’s intentions, ordered his garrison to fire on the advancing people. This sparked a fierce battle. The crowd, driven by desperation and the need for arms, fought their way into the fortress. After hours of intense fighting, the governor was killed by the mob, and the fortress was taken. The crowd discovered the Bastille held only seven prisoners—mostly forgers and a few mentally ill individuals—along with a small stock of gunpowder. The real prize, however, was the gunpowder and the weapons stored inside, which the revolutionaries urgently needed. The fall of the Bastille was less about freeing specific inmates and more about seizing the means to defend their revolution against the king’s military might.

The significance of the event was immediate and profound. The storming of the Bastille became the revolution’s most iconic moment, symbolizing the triumph of popular will over despotism. It proved that the people could act decisively against royal authority, shattering the myth of the monarchy’s invincibility. News of the event spread rapidly across France, inspiring similar uprisings in towns and villages. The National Assembly, now operating under the shadow of this popular uprising, moved swiftly to dismantle the feudal system and issue the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen. The date, July 14th, was later celebrated as France’s national holiday, Bastille Day, a testament to the enduring power of that single day when the people of Paris, acting in their own defense, changed the course of history. The Tennis Court Oath had set the stage for the revolution’s political foundation; the storming of the Bastille made it a reality that could not be ignored, marking the true beginning of the French Revolution’s radical phase.

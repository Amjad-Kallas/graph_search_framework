The Nigerian Civil War, also known as the Biafran War, stemmed from deep-seated ethnic and political fractures in post-colonial Nigeria.

Nigeria gained independence from Britain in 1960, inheriting a federation of diverse ethnic groups—primarily the Hausa-Fulani in the north, Yoruba in the west, and Igbo in the east—along with colonial administrative boundaries that ignored traditional social structures. The war began on July 6, 1967, when federal troops invaded Biafra. The conflict quickly became a brutal struggle characterized by intense artillery barrages, trench warfare, and devastating humanitarian consequences. Biafra’s strategic goal was to establish an independent state for the Igbo people, while Nigeria sought to preserve its territorial integrity, backed by significant military support from the Soviet Union and other African nations.

The Nigerian government’s strategy focused on isolating Biafra militarily and economically, while Biafran forces, though outnumbered and outgunned, held out for nearly three years. The human cost was staggering: an estimated 1 to 3 million Biafrans died from starvation and disease, alongside many Nigerian soldiers and civilians. The war left Nigeria deeply scarred, with lasting ethnic tensions and a legacy of mistrust, particularly between the Igbo and the federal government. It also reinforced the importance of federalism in Nigeria’s governance, though the underlying ethnic divisions remained unresolved. The conflict serves as a stark reminder of how colonial legacies, political miscalculation, and ethnic fear can escalate into catastrophic violence, with repercussions that continue to shape Nigeria’s national identity.

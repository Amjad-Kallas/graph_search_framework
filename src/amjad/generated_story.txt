The French Revolution began on July 14, 1789, with the Storming of the Bastille, marking the end of absolute monarchy in France. Two years later, in February 1791, the Day of Daggers led to the assassination of King Louis XVI, solidifying the National Guard's control over the country. The Haitian Revolution, sparked by Toussaint Louverture, began in August 1791, as enslaved Africans rose up against French colonial rule.

As the French Revolution gained momentum, the country became embroiled in the War of the First Coalition, with various European powers vying for control. The Battle of Croix-des-Bouquets in March 1792 marked a turning point in the Haitian Revolution, while the French army suffered defeats at the hands of the Prussian forces in the Battle of Valmy in September 1792. The Siege of Lille, Thionville, and Mainz followed, as the French army struggled to maintain control over its territories.

The Flanders campaign in November 1792 saw the French army clash with the Austrian and Dutch forces, ultimately leading to the Battle of Jemappes. The French Revolution's international implications continued to unfold, with the French expedition to Sardinia in December 1792 and the East Indies theatre of the French Revolutionary Wars beginning in January 1793. As the French Revolution's fate hung in the balance, the National Convention's Reign of Terror would soon take hold, marking the beginning of a dark period in French history.

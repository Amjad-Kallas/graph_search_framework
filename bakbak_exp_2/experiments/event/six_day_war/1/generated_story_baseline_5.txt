

The Six-Day War of June 1967 emerged from decades of unresolved conflict between Israel and its Arab neighbors, rooted in the 1948 war that established the Jewish state and displaced hundreds of thousands of Palestinians. By 1967, tensions had reached a boiling point, fueled by mutual distrust, competing territorial claims, and a series of escalating military postures. Egypt’s President Gamal Abdel Nasser, seeking to assert Arab unity and challenge Israel’s existence, began mobilizing troops along the Sinai Peninsula border. Crucially, he also blockaded the Straits of Tiran, a vital waterway for Israeli shipping to the port of Eilat, which Israel viewed as an act of war under international law. This blockade, combined with Nasser’s rhetoric and the massing of Egyptian forces near Israel’s border, created a profound sense of existential threat within the Israeli government.

Israel’s leadership, led by Prime Minister Levi Eshkol and Defense Minister Moshe Dayan, assessed that Egypt, Jordan, and Syria were preparing for a coordinated attack. The presence of Egyptian troops in the Sinai, coupled with the closure of the straits, was interpreted as a clear prelude to aggression. Furthermore, the withdrawal of the United Nations Emergency Force (UNEF), which had monitored the Egypt-Israel border since 1957, removed a critical buffer. Israel’s intelligence concluded that waiting for an attack would be catastrophic, as its military was stretched thin and vulnerable to a multi-front assault. Consequently, on the morning of June 5, 1967, the Israeli Air Force launched a devastating preemptive strike against Egyptian airfields, destroying nearly the entire Egyptian air force on the ground within hours. This decisive action, known as Operation Focus, crippled Arab air power and secured Israeli air superiority for the entire conflict.

With air dominance secured, Israeli ground forces swiftly advanced into the Sinai Peninsula. Within three days, they had pushed Egyptian forces back to the Suez Canal, capturing the entire Sinai region. Simultaneously, Jordan, which had signed a defense pact with Egypt, opened fire on Israeli positions in Jerusalem and the West Bank. Israel responded by launching an offensive against Jordanian forces controlling the Old City of Jerusalem and the West Bank. By June 7, Israeli troops had captured East Jerusalem, including the Old City and the Western Wall, and the entire West Bank territory. This victory reunited Jerusalem under Israeli control for the first time since 1948, though it also placed over 500,000 Palestinians under Israeli administration, a situation that would become a central issue in future conflicts.

Meanwhile, Syria, which had been shelling northern Israeli settlements from the Golan Heights, found itself targeted by Israeli forces. After a brief but intense battle, Israeli troops captured the strategically important Golan Heights, a plateau overlooking northern Israel. By June 10, the war had concluded. Israel had achieved a stunning military victory, defeating three Arab armies in just six days, while suffering relatively light casualties compared to its adversaries. The conflict resulted in the occupation of significant territories: the Sinai Peninsula and the Gaza Strip from Egypt, the West Bank and East Jerusalem from Jordan, and the Golan Heights from Syria. These gains dramatically altered the regional map and power balance.

The immediate aftermath of the war was profound. The Arab states suffered a severe blow to their prestige, while Israel, though victorious, faced the complex challenge of administering large populations in the newly acquired territories. The war also triggered a massive refugee crisis, with hundreds of thousands of Palestinians displaced from the West Bank and Gaza, adding to the existing Palestinian refugee population. Crucially, the occupation of the West Bank and East Jerusalem, which had been under Jordanian control since 1948, placed Israel in direct control of areas central to Palestinian national identity and aspirations. The war’s legacy is deeply intertwined with the ongoing Israeli-Palestinian conflict, as the territories captured in 1967 remain at the heart of peace negotiations and political disputes to this day. The Six-Day War thus stands as a pivotal moment that reshaped the Middle East’s political landscape, cementing Israel’s military dominance while simultaneously deepening the roots of the region’s most enduring conflict.
